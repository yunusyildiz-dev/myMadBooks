# Part 6: The Mobile Revolution — 2000s and 2010s

## Computers in Pockets: Symbian and Windows Mobile

In the early 2000s, the smartphone market existed, but "smart" was a relative concept. Nokia's [Symbian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbian) operating system dominated. Limited apps running on small screens, basic email and calendar features — these were "smart," but not ready for touch.

Microsoft's [Windows Mobile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_Mobile) had also stepped onto the stage in the same period. A desktop-like interface crammed onto small screens, a system requiring a stylus. It contained good ideas, but was cumbersome to use.

## The iPhone Earthquake (2007)

On January 9, 2007, Steve Jobs stepped onto the stage at MacWorld in San Francisco and said: **"Today, we're going to reinvent the phone."**

The [iPhone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IPhone_(1st_generation)) was a device that genuinely used the touchscreen, controlled by finger gestures, equipped with an internet browser, and beautiful as a jewel in its design. The [iOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IOS) running beneath it (then called iPhone OS) carried the Unix legacy into pockets via a derivative of Mac OS X.

Competitors initially laughed. Nokia executives said "a phone without a keypad won't sell." BlackBerry co-founder Jim Balsillie said the device wasn't for "real business users." Within two years, the smartphone market had been transformed from the ground up; BlackBerry and Nokia began retreating to the dusty shelves of history.

## Android: Google's Open Answer (2008)

Against the iPhone's closed ecosystem, Google chose a different path: [Android](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Android_(operating_system)). Built on the Linux kernel and offered as open source, Android was open to all phone manufacturers. Samsung, HTC, LG, Sony, Xiaomi — all adopted Android.

This strategy worked. Android today holds approximately **73% of the global smartphone market**. Together with iOS, these two systems control almost the entirety of the modern mobile world. Symbian, Windows Mobile, BlackBerry OS, WebOS — all have passed into history.
