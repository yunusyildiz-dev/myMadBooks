# Part 7: Cloud, Containers, and the Future — 2010s Onward

## Cloud Computing and the Abstraction of the Operating System

With the 2010s came a new paradigm: cloud computing. [Amazon Web Services](https://aws.amazon.com), [Microsoft Azure](https://azure.microsoft.com), and [Google Cloud Platform](https://cloud.google.com) began offering virtual computers independent of physical hardware. You no longer needed to own the hardware hosting an operating system in order to "run" it.

This shift transformed the role of the operating system. Instead of a single large monolithic OS, the concept of "containers" — each fulfilling a small task — gained importance. [Docker](https://www.docker.com) (2013) and [Kubernetes](https://kubernetes.io) (2014) revolutionized this space. An application could now run simultaneously on thousands of servers, scaling automatically.

## Chrome OS and the Web-Centric Approach

In 2011, Google brought a radical idea to life with [Chrome OS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ChromeOS): What if the operating system were just a web browser? Running on devices called Chromebooks, Chrome OS is a lean system that stores its files in the cloud and runs its applications in the browser. It achieved unexpected success in the education sector, becoming the most widely used device in US schools in particular.

## Artificial Intelligence and the New Frontier of the Operating System

By the mid-2020s, operating systems began acquiring an entirely new dimension: **artificial intelligence integration**. With Microsoft's Copilot integrated into Windows, Apple's Apple Intelligence features, and Google's Gemini integration, the operating system is no longer a passive infrastructure — it is transforming into an "assistant" that converses with the user and understands their intentions.

Perhaps in the near future, the concept of the operating system will change fundamentally. Perhaps what we know as the "operating system" will evolve into a software layer — driven by artificial intelligence, shaped according to the task, completely independent of hardware.
