# Part 3: The Personal Computer Revolution — 1970s and 1980s

## The Microprocessor and the Tiny Chip That Changed the World

In 1971, Intel released the first microprocessor, the [4004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intel_4004). This chip, the size of a fingertip, could on its own perform operations previously requiring room-sized computers. This was the moment that opened the doors to computers becoming accessible to everyone.

In 1975, the [MITS Altair 8800](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altair_8800) kit hit the market. It had no screen, no keyboard; it was programmed with switches and gave results through flashing LEDs. But the machine had one crucial virtue: for the first time, it was priced within reach of ordinary people. And it set two young men in motion.

## A Garage, Two Young Men, and CP/M

Paul Allen and Bill Gates trembled with excitement when they heard about the Altair 8800. They wrote a BASIC interpreter for the machine and offered it to MITS. MITS accepted. The foundations of Microsoft (then Micro-Soft) were laid this way.

Around the same time, [Gary Kildall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gary_Kildall) was developing [CP/M](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CP/M) (Control Program for Microcomputers) — the first real personal computer operating system. CP/M achieved enormous success in the late 1970s. Dozens of computer manufacturers licensed CP/M. Kildall was on his way to becoming a billionaire. But then IBM came.

## IBM's Offer and a Historic Mistake (1980)

In 1980, IBM wanted to enter the personal computer market and went to Gary Kildall's company, Digital Research, for an operating system. At precisely this point, history took a controversial turn. Kildall was not at the meeting (some accounts say he had gone flying his plane), his legal team refused to sign the non-disclosure agreement, and negotiations reached an impasse.

IBM knocked on Bill Gates's door next. Gates didn't have an operating system ready, but he found one: He bought [Seattle Computer Products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seattle_Computer_Products)' QDOS (Quick and Dirty Operating System) for $50,000, licensed it to IBM as PC-DOS, and kept the name MS-DOS for his own company.

IBM left the operating system rights with Gates. This became perhaps the most expensive mistake in business history. While IBM PCs were exploding in popularity, every computer sold was fattening Microsoft's coffers. Microsoft today is worth over $3 trillion.

## Apple and the Graphical Interface Dream

In 1979, young Steve Jobs visited [Xerox PARC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PARC_(company)) laboratories. What he saw captivated him: a system that used a computer with a small device called a "mouse" rather than a keyboard, with windows and icons on the screen. This was the [Xerox Alto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xerox_Alto), and Xerox was very reluctant to commercialize it.

> *"Good artists copy; great artists steal." — Steve Jobs*

Apple drew inspiration from this idea and launched the [Lisa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Lisa) in 1983, followed by the [Macintosh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macintosh) in 1984. The Macintosh's launch commercial — the legendary film broadcast during the 1984 Super Bowl with an Orwellian dystopia theme — is still considered one of the greatest advertisements in history.

The Macintosh brought the graphical user interface (GUI) to the masses. You no longer needed to know the command line to use a computer. Clicking on icons was enough. This was one of the greatest turning points in the democratization of computing.

## Microsoft Windows: From Imitation to Market Leader (1985)

Seeing Apple's success, Microsoft developed its own graphical interface: [Windows 1.0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_1.0), released in 1985. The reviews were harsh: slow, ugly, and clunky. Apple filed a lawsuit alleging "theft."

But Microsoft didn't give up. With [Windows 3.0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_3.0) (1990) and then Windows 3.1 (1992), the system became genuinely useful. [Windows 95](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_95), however, was a cultural explosion. Queues formed outside stores, The Rolling Stones' "Start Me Up" played in commercials, and people around the world clicked that iconic **Start** button for the very first time.
