# Part 2: The Birth of Multiplicity — 1960s

## The Time-Sharing Revolution

In the early 1960s, a young researcher at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), [John McCarthy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_McCarthy_(computer_scientist)), put forward a striking idea: Why couldn't multiple users use the same computer simultaneously?

The computers of that era were extraordinarily expensive — the annual lease for an IBM mainframe was equivalent to millions of today's dollars. These machines sat idle much of the time. The time-sharing idea proposed rapidly switching a computer's processing power among multiple users in milliseconds. Users could work as if the entire machine belonged to them.

## CTSS: The First Giant Step (1961)

MIT's [Compatible Time-Sharing System (CTSS)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compatible_Time-Sharing_System), launched in 1961, entered history as the first major example of truly interactive computer use. Users could sit at a terminal, type commands, and receive immediate responses.

Another first that CTSS brought with it: **passwords**. Yes, those passwords that drive all of us mad today entered our lives with CTSS. And right from the very first day, a security vulnerability occurred — due to a system error, the password list of all users was sent to another user. This may well be history's first major "data breach."

## Multics: Big Claims, Bigger Problems (1965–1969)

CTSS's success ignited a grander project: [Multics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multics) (Multiplexed Information and Computing Service). A joint project of MIT, Bell Labs, and General Electric, Multics aimed to incorporate nearly all the features of a modern operating system — secure, multi-user, with a hierarchical file system.

The project was incredibly ambitious — perhaps too ambitious. The system grew increasingly complex, the budget exploded, and performance fell far short of expectations. Bell Labs withdrew from the project in 1969. But this withdrawal planted the seed of an idea that would change world history.

Two Bell Labs engineers who departed from Multics — [Ken Thompson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ken_Thompson) and [Dennis Ritchie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dennis_Ritchie) — used what they had learned from that experience to design a small, simple, elegant system. That system would be **Unix**.

## The Birth of Unix: A Garage Band Legend (1969)

1969 is one of history's inflection points. Neil Armstrong was setting foot on the Moon, Woodstock was gathering tens of thousands, and in a small office at Bell Labs, Ken Thompson was doing something peculiar on a decrepit computer called the PDP-7.

Thompson's wife and son had gone on vacation. During those three weeks, Thompson began using the PDP-7 to play a game called Space Travel — and in the process, laid the foundations of a new operating system. Dennis Ritchie soon joined the project. Ritchie developed a new programming language for this work: **C**.

> *"Unix shaped the way people use computers. It wasn't just an operating system — it was a philosophy: do each thing with small, simple tools; combine these tools to accomplish great things." — Dennis Ritchie*

Unix's philosophy remains valid today. The principle of "do one thing and do it well" is one of the cornerstones of modern software engineering. Linux, macOS, Android, and even some components of modern Windows are built on this venerable legacy.
