# Part 1: The First Light — 1940s and 1950s

## Before the Computer: Mechanical Dreams

Before the 1940s, the word "computer" described not a machine but a human being who performed calculations. There were human computers who drew up large tables and wrestled with slide rules. Then the war came.

World War II forced scientists to develop fast calculating machines. Computing ballistic trajectories for artillery shells, cracking ciphers, processing radar data — all of it demanded tremendous speed.

## ENIAC: The Awakening of Giants (1945)

In 1945, the world's first electronic general-purpose computer — [ENIAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ENIAC) — came to life at the University of Pennsylvania in Philadelphia. Weighing 27 tons, sprawling across 167 square meters, and built from 18,000 vacuum tubes, this colossal machine could perform 5,000 additions per second.

> *"To program ENIAC, you had to manually connect cables running along the walls. Every new calculation required physically reconfiguring the machine. This 'programming' could sometimes take days."*

There is a striking fact about ENIAC's programmers: the first six were all women. [Frances Bilas, Jean Jennings, Ruth Lichterman, Marlyn Wescoff, Frances Snyder, and Betty Holberton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ENIAC#Programmers) — these names spent long years in the shadow of history, but today they are counted among the founders of computer science.

ENIAC had no operating system. If you wanted the machine to do a job, you had to define it directly on the hardware through cable connections. The concept of "software" in the true sense did not yet exist.

## The Von Neumann Architecture and the Stored-Program Concept

At precisely this point, Hungarian-born mathematician [John von Neumann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_von_Neumann) stepped in. In a report he drafted in 1945, he proposed the concept of the "stored program." With this revolutionary idea, a program could be stored in memory alongside data, rather than wiring it physically into the machine.

This seemingly simple idea laid the cornerstone that made the birth of operating systems possible. It was now possible to teach a machine to "think" — with changing programs, for changing tasks.

## The 1950s: Batch Processing and the First OS Stirrings

In the 1950s, companies like IBM began manufacturing large commercial computers. The [IBM 701](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IBM_701) (1952) and the models that followed were used in universities and large corporations. But these machines also ran "bare" — each job required setting the machine up from scratch.

The inefficiency problem was inevitable. Valuable machine time was being wasted on setup and waiting. The solution was found in **batch processing** systems. Jobs punched onto cards were accumulated, fed to the machine in sequence, and results collected in bulk. The rudimentary software developed by [General Motors and North American Aviation in 1956](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_operating_systems#1950s) for the IBM 704 is considered one of the first real examples of an operating system.
