# Part 4: Free Software and the Linux Revolution

## Richard Stallman and the GNU Manifesto (1983)

In 1983, MIT programmer [Richard Stallman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Stallman) penned one of history's most important technological manifestos: he announced the [GNU Project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GNU_Project). According to Stallman, software should be free — not free as in no cost, but free as in freedom. The right to access, modify, and distribute source code should belong to everyone.

Stallman began developing an entirely free Unix-like operating system he called GNU (GNU's Not Unix). Compilers, text editors, utilities — everything was prepared. But one piece was missing: the **kernel**.

🔗 [Read the original GNU Manifesto](https://www.gnu.org/gnu/manifesto.html)

## Linus Torvalds and His "Small" Project (1991)

In 1991, a 21-year-old Finnish university student named [Linus Torvalds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linus_Torvalds) posted a modest message to the `comp.os.minix` newsgroup:

> *"I'm doing a (free) operating system (just a hobby, won't be big and professional like gnu) for 386(486) AT clones..."*

That "small hobby" today forms the foundation of the world's most critical infrastructure. The Linux kernel, combined with Stallman's GNU tools, formed the GNU/Linux operating system. Android phones, 100% of the world's supercomputers, the vast majority of web servers, and billions of embedded systems run on Linux.

Linux's success proved the power of the open-source software model. Thousands of volunteer developers from every corner of the globe improve the system, fix bugs, and patch security vulnerabilities. Independent of any single company, this community-owned model changed the paradigm of software development.
